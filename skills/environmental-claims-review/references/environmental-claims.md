# Environmental claims issue-spotting reference

This reference supports preliminary review of environmental claims in WMF copy and creative. It is not legal advice or a substitute for counsel, local regulatory review, substantiation, or approval by the claim owner.

## First question: where will the claim appear?

Record the target market, language, product category, channel, publication date, and whether the claim applies to the product, packaging, a component, manufacturing, delivery, or the whole brand. A claim that is acceptable in one market or context may not be acceptable in another.

If the market is unknown, report the uncertainty and use a conservative screen. Do not give a legal pass.

## Claim inventory

Review all visible and implied environmental signals:

- Words: green, eco-friendly, sustainable, planet-friendly, climate positive, carbon neutral, net zero, zero impact, renewable, natural, non-toxic, recyclable, compostable, biodegradable, recycled, refillable, low waste, energy efficient, durable, made to last, responsible, or better for the planet.
- Comparisons: less impact, lower carbon, reduced waste, greener, more sustainable, or better than the previous model or a competitor.
- Symbols and presentation: leaves, earths, green palettes, certification seals, chasing arrows, carbon icons, natural imagery, or product names that imply environmental benefits.
- Footnotes and interactions: qualifiers hidden below the fold, behind accordions, in tooltips, or separated from the claim.

## Minimum substantiation questions

For each material claim, ask:

1. What exact environmental attribute is being claimed?
2. Does it apply to the product, packaging, a component, a process, or the brand overall?
3. What evidence supports it, using what test, lifecycle boundary, baseline, and date?
4. Is the comparison like-for-like and clearly stated?
5. Are limitations, geographic availability, disposal conditions, or required consumer behaviour clear and prominent?
6. Could the overall presentation imply a broader benefit than the evidence supports?
7. Is a certification or logo genuine, current, and accompanied by accessible criteria?

## High-risk patterns

- Broad unqualified environmental benefits: “green”, “eco-friendly”, “sustainable”, or “planet-friendly”. Prefer a specific, evidenced attribute.
- Absolute claims: “zero impact”, “100% sustainable”, “carbon neutral”, or “climate positive” without complete scope, methodology, and current evidence.
- Disposal claims: “recyclable”, “compostable”, or “biodegradable” without material scope, conditions, time period, and realistic facility access.
- Material claims: “made with recycled” or “renewable” without percentage, component scope, and a clear description of the material.
- Energy claims: “made with renewable energy” without identifying the energy source, manufacturing scope, time period, and relevant certificates or evidence.
- Offset claims: “carbon neutral” based only on offsets, without evidence of the underlying emissions calculation and claims presentation.
- Comparative claims: “less waste” or “lower impact” without a named comparator, consistent method, and quantified basis.
- Green visuals that imply a general benefit while the copy only proves a narrow attribute.

## Review posture by market

### EU / EEA

The EU consumer-empowerment rules for the green transition prohibit vague environmental claims without demonstrated recognised excellent environmental performance and restrict unreliable voluntary sustainability logos. The European Commission states that Member States were required to transpose the rules by 27 March 2026 and that they apply from 27 September 2026. Verify the national implementation and the relevant product rules for the target market before publication.

### United Kingdom

Use the CMA Green Claims Code as an issue-spotting baseline: claims should be truthful and accurate, clear and unambiguous, not omit material information, compare fairly, consider the full lifecycle where relevant, and be substantiated. The CMA’s collection was updated on 22 January 2026, including supply-chain guidance.

### United States

Use the FTC Green Guides as an issue-spotting baseline. Broad unqualified claims such as “green” or “eco-friendly” are high risk; specific qualifications should be clear, prominent, and specific. Claims such as recyclable, compostable, degradable, recycled content, renewable materials, renewable energy, non-toxic, and carbon offsets have additional substantiation concerns.

## Report language

Use “potential environmental-claims risk” or “requires substantiation/legal review”, not “illegal”, unless qualified counsel or the relevant authority has made that determination. Always state what evidence was unavailable and what exact change would reduce the risk.
